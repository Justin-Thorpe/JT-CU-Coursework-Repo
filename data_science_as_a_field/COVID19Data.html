global_top_10 <- global_top_10 %>%
  mutate(CFR = (deaths / cases) * 100) #this is a infection to death ratio each day

This model shows that over time there was a large increase in the case fatalities initially, with a slow leveling off for the remaining several years.

I can conclude that over the course of COVID 19, infections rose quickly but eventually leveled off, meanwhile the fatality rate actually decreased over time. I would need more data to make any assumptions about vaccination rates to add that to a model.

Some biases I can make out with this data are as follows: 1. Testing bias and the under reporting of cases. Not all countries reported uniformly, and the capacity for accurate testing was limited. So we may never know the true number of cases. 2. Attribution bias, where deaths were reported as being due to COVID-19 even if it may have been another cause. This could have inflated the fatality rate. Only by retroactively going through cases would you be able to better attribute the deaths. 3. Geographic bias since there are more people in cities, thus it can be easy to assume developed nations and places with more urban density would be over represented than in rural communities.
